Imraan's poems from Zindagi Na Milegi Dobara — full text and what they really mean

Farhan Akhtar wrote words that a whole generation carried with them. Here they are, in full, with everything they carry.

By Sahebjit Chhabra  •  lostintrovert.com  •  April 2026

There is a specific kind of person who remembers exactly where they were when they first heard Imraan recite a poem in Zindagi Na Milegi Dobara. Not the song. Not the dialogue. The poems.

Written by Farhan Akhtar — who also played Imraan — the poems are the emotional spine of the film. While Kabir chases control and Arjun runs from his past, Imraan carries poetry in his pocket like a quiet weapon. He takes it out at exactly the right moments. And somehow, each time, it says the thing you didn't know you needed to hear.

This video has over 1.3 million views. That number tells you something. People aren't just watching — they're returning. They're saving it. They're sending it to someone at 2am.

Here is every poem Imraan recites in the film, in full, with translation and context.

The poems, in order

Poem 1 — Dilon mein tum apni betabiyan leke chalo

Dilon mein tum apni betabiyan leke chalo,
Agar kahin hai ujala to roshni leke chalo.
Mujhe yakeen hai ke manzil milegi zaroor,
Chalo tum bhi apni zindagi leke chalo.
Carry your restlessness in your heart,
If there is light somewhere, carry that light.
I am certain the destination will be found,
Come, carry your life with you too.
Recited early in the journey — an invocation

This is the poem that opens everything. Imraan recites it almost to himself, but it's really addressed to both his friends and to us. Betabiyan — restlessness, impatience, longing — is framed not as a burden but as something to carry proudly. The poem is saying: your hunger is not your weakness. Bring it.

Poem 2 — Seene mein jalan aankhon mein toofan sa kyun hai

Seene mein jalan, aankhon mein toofan sa kyun hai,
Is shehar mein har shakhs pareshan sa kyun hai.
Dil hai toh dhadakne ka bahana dhundhega,
Pathar ki tarah chupchaap pighalna kyun hai.
Why is there a burning in the chest, a storm in the eyes,
Why does every person in this city seem troubled.
If there is a heart, it will find a reason to beat,
Why melt silently like stone.
Recited during a moment of stillness in the journey

This one hits differently depending on where you are in life. The burning chest, the stormy eyes — these are the symptoms of being alive and unsatisfied. But the turn in the third line is everything: Dil hai toh dhadakne ka bahana dhundhega. If you have a heart, it will find a reason to beat. It's not advice. It's a reminder. The heart doesn't need permission.

Poem 3 — Aisa kyun hota hai, aisa kyun hota hai

Aisa kyun hota hai, aisa kyun hota hai,
Jo khona chahte hain woh khota nahin.
Aur jo chahte hain paana woh milta nahin,
Zindagi ka yeh kaisa silsila hota hai.
Why does this happen, why does this happen,
What we want to lose, we cannot lose.
And what we want to find, we cannot find,
What kind of order is this, in life.
The most human of the three — recited when the trip begins to fracture

The simplest poem. No metaphor, no ornament. Just a question asked four different ways. And that's what makes it devastating. Jo khona chahte hain woh khota nahin — what we want to lose, stays. The guilt. The memory. The version of yourself you're trying to leave behind. Farhan wrote this like someone who had actually tried.

Why these poems matter

ZNMD is a film about three men running from themselves at high speed through Spain. The diving, the tomatoes, the bulls — it's all beautiful distraction. But Imraan's poems are the film's conscience. They slow everything down. They ask the question the action is trying to avoid.

Farhan Akhtar wrote these himself. Not a poet brought in for the job — the actor, the director's friend, the man on screen, wrote the words his character would speak. That specificity is why they land. They're not written to sound poetic. They're written because someone needed to say them.

The 1.3 million people who've watched this video aren't there for the cinematography. They're there because at some point, one of these poems said exactly what they were feeling and couldn't find the words for.

That's what good poetry does. It doesn't teach you something new. It names what you already knew.
